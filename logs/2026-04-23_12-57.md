## DEV

Title: Systems don’t fail at execution. They fail before anyone is looking.

What is happening

Most systems are evaluated at output.
If results look correct, the system is assumed stable.

But behavior is not static.
It shifts across repeated interactions.

What looks stable in a single moment can degrade over time.

What it means

A system is not defined by a single response.
It is defined by patterns across time.

When those patterns shift without intervention,
Behavioral Drift forms.

No alarms fire.
No thresholds are crossed.

But the system is no longer aligned.

What breaks

Most governance operates after the fact.

Monitoring sees it.
Audit reconstructs it.
Review explains it.

But none of these stop it.

So drift accumulates.
And Longitudinal Risk grows without visibility.

What to do

Governance must operate during execution.

Decision Boundary must be enforced before continuation.

Intervention Threshold must exist before deviation compounds.

Stop Authority must be real, not theoretical.

Execution Example

Repeated low-risk deviations occur.

Each one is acceptable in isolation.

No escalation triggers.

Over time, the system normalizes the deviation.

Outcome:
The system operates as designed, but no longer behaves as intended.

## Comment

This is the gap most people miss.

The system can look stable at execution while drifting underneath.

Outputs stay clean.
So nothing triggers action.

Simple test:

Is there a real Decision Boundary during execution
or just visibility after the fact

That difference determines whether drift is contained
or allowed to accumulate
